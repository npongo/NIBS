Potential for reducing greenhouse gas emissions from cropland in India: where, Which(crop), and Who(farmers)

Abstract

India is the largest emitter in the world of greenhouse gases from agriculture greenhouse gas (GHG) and is dominated by small-scale farmers who are highly vulnerable to climate change. To help target interventions that reduce emissions while benefiting farmers, we estimate GHG emissions for 44 crops at a district level using an ensemble of models for three components linked to clear management actions: nitrous-oxide linked to nutrient management, methane linked to water management and organic input in rice and residue management linked to burning emissions. Results vary across districts, suggesting a tailored rather than one-size-fits-all approach for interventions. Although rice, wheat and maize are the dominant per hectare emitting crops, green gram and rapeseed show high nitrous oxide emissions relative to yield, offering opportunities to reduce both emissions and improve farm profits. Rice production is the largest source of GHG emissions; improving water management to reduce methane would have multiple co-benefits by reducing water stress at the landscape scale. Small and marginal farms (<2Ha) have the highest emissions of all farm sizes because they grow a less diverse set of crops focused primarily on rice and apply higher rates of organic inputs for fertilizer. Improving utilization of organic inputs and diversifying crops for small and marginal farms would help reduce emissions while improving climate resilience and livelihoods. The data from this paper has the potential to help develop district level profiles for targeted intervention to reduce cropland GHG.
